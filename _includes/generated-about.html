Fabio Giglietto, PhD, is a renowned Professor of Internet Studies at the Department of Communication Sciences, Humanities and International Studies at the University of Urbino Carlo Bo. With a career dedicated to exploring the intricate relationships between social systems, media, and digital technologies, Professor Giglietto has established himself as a leading figure in the field of Internet studies. His research delves deeply into the theory of information, communication, and society, making significant contributions to understanding how these dynamics influence our world.

Professor Giglietto's scholarly work is widely published in esteemed journals, including the Journal of Communication, Information, Communication & Society, and Social Media + Society. Among his notable publications are "Second Screen and Participation: A Content Analysis on a Full Season Dataset of Tweets," and "The open laboratory: Limits and possibilities of using Facebook, Twitter, and YouTube as a research data source," reflecting his interest in how digital platforms serve as both research tools and subjects. His research has garnered over 1,740 citations, underscoring the impact and importance of his contributions to the field.

Currently, Professor Giglietto is actively engaged in research that addresses the challenges and opportunities presented by social media analysis and disinformation. His recent projects involve examining the coordinated behaviors in media manipulation and the circulation of false information within hybrid news systems. Through these efforts, he aims to provide insights into the evolving landscape of digital communication and its implications for society. His active presence on platforms such as BlueSky and Mastodon highlights his commitment to staying at the forefront of discussions on computational social science and digital media literacy.

In addition to his research, Professor Giglietto is deeply involved in academic and professional communities, contributing to advancements in the field through collaborations and public dialogues. His work not only advances academic knowledge but also informs policy and practice, making a lasting impact on both scholarly and public domains.

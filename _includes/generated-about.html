Fabio Giglietto, PhD, is an esteemed Associate Professor of Internet Studies at the Department of Communication Sciences, Humanities and International Studies at the University of Urbino Carlo Bo. With a profound commitment to understanding the intricate dynamics between social systems, media, and digital technologies, Professor Giglietto has established himself as a prominent scholar in the field. His research delves into the theory of information, communication, and society, exploring how these elements intersect and influence each other in the digital age.

Professor Giglietto has contributed significantly to the academic community with his extensive publications in leading journals such as the Journal of Communication, Information, Communication & Society, and Social Media + Society. His work, including influential papers like "Second Screen and Participation: A Content Analysis on a Full Season Dataset of Tweets" and "‘Fake news’ is the invention of a liar: How false information circulates within the hybrid news system," demonstrates his expertise in media manipulation and the social implications of digital communication. His research has accumulated a notable 1751 citations, reflecting the impact of his scholarly contributions.

Currently, Professor Giglietto is actively engaged in research projects that examine social media analysis and disinformation, with a focus on computational social science. His recent activities on platforms such as BlueSky and Mastodon highlight his ongoing interest in the evolving landscape of digital communication and its societal impacts. Through his work, he continues to explore the potential and challenges of using digital platforms as research tools, as evidenced by publications like "The open laboratory: Limits and possibilities of using Facebook, Twitter, and YouTube as a research data source."

In addition to his research, Professor Giglietto is dedicated to fostering academic growth and collaboration at the University of Urbino Carlo Bo. He integrates his cutting-edge research into his teaching, guiding students through the complexities of Internet Studies and preparing them to address the pressing issues of digital communication in contemporary society. His contributions to both academia and public discourse underscore his commitment to advancing our understanding of the digital world.

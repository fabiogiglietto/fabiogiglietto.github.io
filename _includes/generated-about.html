Fabio Giglietto, PhD, is an esteemed Professor of Internet Studies at the University of Urbino Carlo Bo, where he is a leading faculty member in the Department of Communication Sciences, Humanities and International Studies. With a robust academic background, Professor Giglietto's work primarily explores the intricate interplay between social systems, media, and digital technologies. His research delves into the theory of information and communication, providing critical insights into the societal impacts of digital transformation.

Professor Giglietto's scholarly contributions are extensive and highly influential, with his research being published in renowned journals such as the Journal of Communication and Social Media + Society. Among his notable works are "Second Screen and Participation: A Content Analysis on a Full Season Dataset of Tweets," which has garnered significant academic attention with 397 citations, and "The open laboratory: Limits and possibilities of using Facebook, Twitter, and YouTube as a research data source," cited 276 times. His research often addresses contemporary issues, such as the spread of disinformation and media manipulation, exemplified by his study on coordinated link sharing during Italian elections.

Currently, Professor Giglietto is actively advancing his research on social media analysis and the dynamics of disinformation within the hybrid news ecosystem. His recent work continues to explore how false information proliferates online, a topic of growing importance in today's digital landscape. He engages with the academic community and broader public through platforms such as BlueSky, where he shares insights and developments in his research endeavors.

In addition to his academic pursuits, Professor Giglietto has been involved in several high-impact research projects and has received accolades for his contributions to the field. His dedication to advancing the understanding of digital media's role in society is reflected in his ongoing projects and collaborations, positioning him as a thought leader in computational social science and internet studies.

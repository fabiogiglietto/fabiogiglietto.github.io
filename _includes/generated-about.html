Fabio Giglietto, PhD, is a distinguished Associate Professor of Internet Studies at the University of Urbino Carlo Bo, where he is an integral part of the Department of Communication Sciences, Humanities and International Studies. With a deep-seated passion for understanding the intricate dynamics between social systems, media, and digital technologies, Professor Giglietto is at the forefront of research in information theory and communication. His work is pivotal in exploring the impact of digital platforms on society, and he consistently contributes to leading journals such as the Journal of Communication, Information, Communication & Society, and Social Media + Society.

Professor Giglietto's prolific research career is underscored by numerous impactful publications, including his highly cited work "Second Screen and Participation," which delves into the intersection of social media and participatory behavior. His research extends to the critical analysis of disinformation and media manipulation, as evidenced by his studies on coordinated link sharing during Italian elections and the propagation of false information within hybrid news systems. His keen insights into these areas make him a sought-after voice in the realm of computational social science and social media analysis.

In addition to his academic contributions, Professor Giglietto actively engages with the broader academic community through platforms like BlueSky and Mastodon, where he shares his latest research endeavors and insights into current projects. His recent work continues to focus on the challenges and opportunities presented by social media as a research data source, as well as the societal implications of digital communication trends. Through these efforts, he is committed to advancing the understanding of digital media's role in shaping contemporary communication practices.

Professor Giglietto's dedication to his field is further exemplified through his involvement in cutting-edge research projects and collaborations that address pressing issues in media and society. He remains a pivotal figure in the academic community, influencing both current and future scholars in the ever-evolving landscape of internet studies.

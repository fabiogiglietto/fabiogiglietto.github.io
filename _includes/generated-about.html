Fabio Giglietto, PhD, is an esteemed Associate Professor of Internet Studies at the University of Urbino Carlo Bo, within the Department of Communication Sciences, Humanities and International Studies. With a keen interest in the intersection of social systems, media, and digital technologies, Professor Giglietto's work delves deeply into the theory of information, communication, and society. His scholarly contributions have been widely recognized, with publications in renowned journals such as the Journal of Communication, Information, Communication & Society, and Social Media + Society.

Professor Giglietto's research portfolio is extensive and influential, with significant works including "Second Screen and Participation: A Content Analysis on a Full Season Dataset of Tweets" and "The open laboratory: Limits and possibilities of using Facebook, Twitter, and YouTube as a research data source," which have collectively garnered over 1,700 citations. His recent studies focus on the dynamics of misinformation and coordinated media manipulation, particularly within the context of electoral processes in Italy, which underscores his commitment to understanding and addressing issues of disinformation in the digital age.

In addition to his academic endeavors, Professor Giglietto is actively engaged in various research projects that explore the nuances of social media analysis and computational social science. His latest activities, as shared on platforms such as BlueSky and LinkedIn, highlight his ongoing projects aimed at developing innovative methodologies for analyzing digital communication. These initiatives not only contribute to academic discourse but also offer practical insights into the evolving landscape of digital interactions.

Through his dedication to research and teaching, Fabio Giglietto continues to shape the field of Internet Studies, providing valuable perspectives on how digital technologies influence contemporary society. His work not only enriches the academic community but also informs public understanding of the complex relationships between media, technology, and social behavior.

Fabio Giglietto, PhD, is a distinguished Professor of Internet Studies in the Department of Communication Sciences, Humanities and International Studies at the University of Urbino Carlo Bo. With a deep-seated interest in the theory of information, communication, and society, Professor Giglietto focuses his research on the intricate relationships between social systems, media, and digital technologies. His scholarly work has been widely published, featuring in renowned journals such as the Journal of Communication, Information, Communication & Society, and Social Media + Society.

Professor Giglietto's academic contributions are substantial, with notable publications including "Second Screen and Participation: A Content Analysis on a Full Season Dataset of Tweets," which has garnered significant attention with 399 citations. His work, "The open laboratory: Limits and possibilities of using Facebook, Twitter, and YouTube as a research data source," further exemplifies his commitment to exploring the role of digital platforms in modern research. In his recent studies, he has delved into coordinated link sharing behavior during Italian elections and the dissemination of false information within hybrid news systems, reflecting his ongoing commitment to understanding media manipulation and disinformation.

Currently, Professor Giglietto is actively involved in research projects that examine the impact of social media on public discourse and the spread of disinformation. His recent activities on platforms such as BlueSky and Mastodon indicate a keen interest in computational social science and social media analysis. Through these channels, he engages with a broader audience, discussing his latest findings and collaborative projects that push the boundaries of internet studies.

In addition to his research endeavors, Professor Giglietto plays a pivotal role in academia as an educator, teaching courses on Internet Studies at the University of Urbino Carlo Bo. His academic leadership and innovative research continue to influence the field, making significant strides in understanding the digital landscape's complexities and its societal implications.

Fabio Giglietto, PhD, is a distinguished Associate Professor of Internet Studies at the University of Urbino Carlo Bo, where he is a pivotal figure in the Department of Communication Sciences, Humanities and International Studies. With a profound expertise in social media analysis, computational social science, and digital methods, he delves deeply into the intricate relationships between social systems, media, and digital technologies. His pedagogical endeavors include teaching Internet Studies, where he fosters an environment of critical engagement and innovation among his students.

Professor Giglietto’s research has been instrumental in advancing our understanding of information theory, communication, and society. His scholarly contributions have been featured in leading academic journals such as the Journal of Communication and Information, Communication & Society. Notable publications include "Second Screen and Participation: A Content Analysis on a Full Season Dataset of Tweets," which has garnered significant academic attention with 397 citations. His work often explores the dynamics of media manipulation and disinformation, exemplified by studies like "It takes a village to manipulate the media: coordinated link sharing behavior during 2018 and 2019 Italian elections."

Engaged in both theoretical and applied research, Dr. Giglietto’s recent projects focus on the impact of digital technologies on the dissemination of information and the challenges posed by information disorder. His active participation in international conferences and collaborations highlights his commitment to addressing contemporary issues in media studies. Online, he shares insights and engages with the academic community through platforms such as BlueSky and Mastodon, where he discusses current projects and developments in the field.

With a total of 20 publications and an impressive citation count of 1740, Fabio Giglietto continues to contribute to the academic community with impactful research. He remains dedicated to exploring the limits and possibilities of digital platforms as research data sources, as demonstrated in his widely-cited paper "The open laboratory: Limits and possibilities of using Facebook, Twitter, and YouTube as a research data source." His ongoing work not only enriches the academic discourse but also informs public understanding of digital communication's evolving landscape.

Fabio Giglietto, PhD, is a distinguished Professor of Internet Studies at the University of Urbino Carlo Bo, where he serves within the Department of Communication Sciences, Humanities and International Studies. With a deep-seated interest in the theory of information, communication, and society, Professor Giglietto's work primarily explores the intricate relationships between social systems, media, and digital technologies. His scholarly contributions have been widely recognized, with publications featured in leading journals such as the Journal of Communication, Information, Communication & Society, and Social Media + Society.

Professor Giglietto has made significant strides in understanding the dynamics of social media and disinformation, and his highly cited works include "Second Screen and Participation: A Content Analysis on a Full Season Dataset of Tweets" and "The open laboratory: Limits and possibilities of using Facebook, Twitter, and YouTube as a research data source." His research sheds light on how digital platforms can be both a source of innovative data collection and a challenge due to issues like media manipulation, as detailed in his study on coordinated link sharing behavior during the Italian elections.

Beyond his academic publications, Professor Giglietto is actively engaged in contemporary research projects that delve into the complexities of computational social science and the spread of false information within hybrid news systems. His recent work on hashtag discourses post-Charlie Hebdo demonstrates his commitment to understanding and dissecting online narratives. His insights and updates on ongoing projects are frequently shared on professional networks like LinkedIn and emerging platforms such as BlueSky, where he continues to influence the discourse on social media analysis and digital communication.

At the University of Urbino Carlo Bo, Professor Giglietto is not only a prolific researcher but also a dedicated educator, mentoring the next generation of scholars in Internet Studies. His contributions to the academic community have been acknowledged through numerous citations and collaborations, making him a pivotal figure in the study of digital society. Through both his teaching and research, he remains at the forefront of exploring how digital technologies reshape societal interactions and communication practices.

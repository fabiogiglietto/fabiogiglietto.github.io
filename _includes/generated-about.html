Fabio Giglietto, PhD, is an esteemed Professor of Internet Studies at the University of Urbino Carlo Bo, where he also serves in the Department of Communication Sciences, Humanities and International Studies. His academic journey is marked by a deep commitment to exploring the intersections of social systems, media, and digital technologies. As an expert in the theory of information, communication, and society, Professor Giglietto's research provides valuable insights into how digital environments influence societal dynamics.

Throughout his career, Professor Giglietto has made significant contributions to the field through his extensive publications in prestigious journals such as the Journal of Communication, Information, Communication & Society, and Social Media + Society. His work often delves into the complexities of social media analysis and the dissemination of information, shedding light on phenomena like 'fake news' and media manipulation. His notable studies, such as "Second Screen and Participation" and "The open laboratory," have garnered several hundred citations, underlining his influence in the academic community.

Currently, Professor Giglietto is deeply engaged in projects that examine the role of coordinated link sharing behavior in media manipulation, using computational social science methods. His research is not only theoretical but also has practical implications, particularly in understanding electoral processes and information circulation in contemporary digital landscapes. His recent endeavors have drawn attention to the nuanced strategies employed in digital communication and the importance of safeguarding the integrity of information systems.

In addition to his academic pursuits, Professor Giglietto actively shares his insights and developments in his research through platforms like BlueSky, Mastodon, and LinkedIn. His recent posts reflect a keen interest in addressing disinformation and enhancing the methodologies used in social media analysis. As a respected voice in his field, he continues to contribute to the academic and public discourse on the critical issues surrounding media and technology today.

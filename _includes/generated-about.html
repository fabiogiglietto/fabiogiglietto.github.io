Fabio Giglietto, PhD, is a distinguished Professor of Internet Studies at the Department of Communication Sciences, Humanities and International Studies at the University of Urbino Carlo Bo. With a profound interest in the theory of information, communication, and society, Professor Giglietto's work predominantly explores the intricate relationships between social systems, media, and digital technologies. His extensive research has been featured in prominent journals such as the Journal of Communication, Information, Communication & Society, and Social Media + Society, establishing him as a leading voice in his field.

Throughout his career, Professor Giglietto has published numerous influential papers, including "Second Screen and Participation: A Content Analysis on a Full Season Dataset of Tweets," which has garnered significant academic attention with 397 citations. His research on the use of platforms like Facebook, Twitter, and YouTube as research data sources has also been pivotal, shedding light on the potential and limitations of these digital landscapes. Notably, his work on coordinated link sharing during Italian elections and the circulation of false information within hybrid news systems underscores his commitment to understanding the dynamics of media manipulation and disinformation.

In his current role, Professor Giglietto continues to delve into the complexities of social media analysis and computational social science. He is actively engaged in exploring mechanisms of disinformation and the impact of digital technologies on societal communication. Recently, his efforts have been bolstered by new research projects and collaborations, reflecting his ongoing dedication to advancing knowledge in Internet Studies. His active presence on platforms like BlueSky and LinkedIn further illustrates his engagement with contemporary issues in digital communication and his commitment to fostering meaningful discourse in the academic community.

Professor Giglietto's contributions to the field have been recognized not only through his extensive publication record, which boasts a total of 20 publications and 1756 citations, but also through his active participation in academic conferences and workshops. His work continues to influence both scholars and practitioners, as he remains at the forefront of research on the societal implications of digital technologies.

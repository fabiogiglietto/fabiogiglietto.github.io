Fabio Giglietto, PhD, is an esteemed Associate Professor of Internet Studies at the Department of Communication Sciences, Humanities and International Studies at the University of Urbino Carlo Bo. With a focus on the intricate relationships between social systems, media, and digital technologies, Professor Giglietto has made significant contributions to the field through his extensive research and publications. His work, which explores the theory of information, communication, and society, has been featured in leading academic journals such as the Journal of Communication, Information, Communication & Society, and Social Media + Society.

Professor Giglietto's research interests are centered around social media analysis, disinformation, and computational social science. His notable publications include "Second Screen and Participation: A Content Analysis on a Full Season Dataset of Tweets," which has been cited 397 times, and "The open laboratory: Limits and possibilities of using Facebook, Twitter, and YouTube as a research data source," with 276 citations. These works highlight his dedication to understanding the evolving dynamics of media and technology in contemporary society.

In addition to his academic endeavors, Professor Giglietto actively engages with the scholarly community and the public through various platforms, including BlueSky, Mastodon, Threads, and LinkedIn. His recent activities reflect an ongoing commitment to exploring how false information circulates within hybrid news systems, as seen in his publication "‘Fake news’ is the invention of a liar." He continues to lead research projects that address critical issues surrounding media manipulation and coordinated online behavior, particularly in the context of political events such as the Italian elections.

Through his role at the University of Urbino Carlo Bo, Fabio Giglietto remains at the forefront of internet studies, providing valuable insights into the complex interactions between digital technologies and societal structures. His work not only advances academic discourse but also informs policy and public understanding of the digital age's challenges and opportunities.

Fabio Giglietto, Ph.D., is a distinguished Professor of Internet Studies at the University of Urbino Carlo Bo's Department of Communication Sciences, Humanities and International Studies. He is renowned for his extensive research on information theory and the intricate relationships between social systems, media, and digital technologies. With a prolific publication record in esteemed journals such as the Journal of Communication, Information, Communication & Society, and Social Media + Society, his work significantly contributes to understanding the dynamics of digital communication in contemporary society.

Professor Giglietto's research delves into the impact of social media on news dissemination and the propagation of disinformation within hybrid news systems. His notable studies include "Second Screen and Participation," which offers valuable insights into audience engagement via social media, and "It takes a village to manipulate the media," exploring coordinated online behaviors during pivotal political events. His scholarly contributions, totaling over 20 publications with 1740 citations, underscore his influence in the field of Internet studies.

At the University of Urbino Carlo Bo, Professor Giglietto also leads projects focusing on social media analysis and computational social science, reflecting his ongoing commitment to advancing academic understanding in these areas. His recent research initiatives include examining the role of digital platforms in shaping public discourse and the challenges posed by disinformation. Through his active presence on platforms like BlueSky and LinkedIn, he engages with both the academic community and the public, sharing insights and fostering discussions on the evolving landscape of digital communication.

Professor Giglietto's academic excellence is complemented by his participation in collaborative international research endeavors and his contribution to shaping future communication strategies in the digital age. His dedication to exploring the intersections of technology and society positions him as a leading figure in the ongoing discourse surrounding digital media's transformative impact on global communication networks.

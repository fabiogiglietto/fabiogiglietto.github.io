Fabio Giglietto, PhD, is a distinguished Professor of Internet Studies at the University of Urbino Carlo Bo, where he is an integral part of the Department of Communication Sciences, Humanities and International Studies. As an associate professor, he imparts his extensive knowledge in Internet Studies, focusing on the intricate relationships between social systems, media, and digital technologies. With a robust academic background, Professor Giglietto's research ambitiously bridges the theory of information, communication, and society, contributing significantly to the field.

Professor Giglietto has an impressive portfolio of research work, having published extensively in renowned journals such as the Journal of Communication, Information, Communication & Society, and Social Media + Society. His pioneering studies include insightful analyses of social media dynamics and the dissemination of information, exemplified by works like "Second Screen and Participation: A Content Analysis on a Full Season Dataset of Tweets," which has garnered significant academic citations. His research delves into contemporary issues such as media manipulation and disinformation, with notable publications addressing these challenges in the digital age.

Beyond his academic contributions, Professor Giglietto is actively engaged in ongoing research projects that explore the realms of social media analysis and computational social science. His recent endeavors include examining coordinated behaviors in digital communication, particularly during critical events like elections. As a thought leader in the field, he frequently shares his insights and latest research findings through platforms such as BlueSky and Mastodon, fostering a broader understanding of digital media impacts on society.

Professor Giglietto's work is not only academically rigorous but also socially impactful, earning him recognition and citations across numerous scholarly articles. His commitment to advancing the study of digital communications continues to influence both academia and the wider public discourse, positioning him as a pivotal figure in Internet Studies and media research.

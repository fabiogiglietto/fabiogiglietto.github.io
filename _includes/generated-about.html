Fabio Giglietto, PhD, is an Associate Professor of Internet Studies at the Department of Communication Sciences, Humanities and International Studies at the University of Urbino Carlo Bo. With a deep-seated expertise in the theory of information, communication, and society, his work primarily explores the intricate relationships between social systems, media, and digital technologies. Fabio has made significant contributions to the field through his extensive publications in prestigious journals such as the Journal of Communication, Information, Communication & Society, and Social Media + Society.

Over the years, Professor Giglietto has authored 20 publications, garnering a total of 1740 citations, reflecting the impact of his research on topics such as second screen participation, media manipulation, and the dynamics of false information within hybrid news systems. Some of his most cited works include "Second Screen and Participation: A Content Analysis on a Full Season Dataset of Tweets" and "The open laboratory: Limits and possibilities of using Facebook, Twitter, and YouTube as a research data source."

Currently, Fabio is actively engaged in cutting-edge research projects that focus on social media analysis, disinformation, and computational social science. His recent work delves into understanding coordinated link-sharing behaviors and the circulation of false information during critical events such as elections. Through his presence on platforms like BlueSky and Mastodon, he shares insights into his ongoing projects, fostering academic and public discourse on the role of digital technologies in contemporary society.

Professor Giglietto's dedication to advancing knowledge in his field is also evident through his involvement in numerous grants and professional activities, making him a pivotal figure in the academic community. His current endeavors continue to shape our understanding of the digital landscape, emphasizing the importance of rigorous analysis in the age of information and digital communication.

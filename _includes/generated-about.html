Fabio Giglietto, PhD, is an esteemed Associate Professor of Internet Studies at the Department of Communication Sciences, Humanities and International Studies at the University of Urbino Carlo Bo. With a deep-seated passion for exploring the complexities of digital communication, Professor Giglietto's work delves into the intricate relationships between social systems, media, and digital technologies. His scholarly contributions have been published in prestigious journals such as the Journal of Communication, Information, Communication & Society, and the Journal of Broadcasting and Electronic Media.

Professor Giglietto's prolific research agenda includes a focus on social media analysis and the spread of disinformation, evidenced by his widely cited works like "Second Screen and Participation: A Content Analysis on a Full Season Dataset of Tweets" and "‘Fake news’ is the invention of a liar: How false information circulates within the hybrid news system." His ability to illuminate the dynamics of media manipulation, particularly in the context of political events such as the Italian elections, underscores his impactful contributions to the field of Internet Studies.

In addition to his academic pursuits, Professor Giglietto actively engages with the academic community and the public through platforms such as BlueSky, Mastodon, and LinkedIn, where he shares insights and developments in his ongoing research projects. His current work continues to advance the understanding of computational social science and the role of digital platforms in shaping public discourse. Through recent research endeavors and collaborations, he remains at the forefront of discussions on the societal implications of digital communication technologies.

Professor Giglietto's dedication to both teaching and research is reflected in his commitment to fostering a nuanced understanding of media and technology among his students and peers. His ongoing projects and academic contributions have been recognized through numerous citations and a growing body of influential publications, cementing his reputation as a leading figure in the study of digital communication.

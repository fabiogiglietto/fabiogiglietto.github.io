Fabio Giglietto, PhD, is a Full Professor of Internet Studies at the University of Urbino Carlo Bo, where he is part of the Department of Communication Sciences, Humanities and International Studies. With a deep expertise in the theory of information, communication, and society, his research primarily explores the intersections between social systems, media, and digital technologies. Professor Giglietto is renowned for his scholarly contributions to understanding how digital platforms influence societal dynamics and the dissemination of information.

Professor Giglietto has an impressive publication record, featuring extensively in prestigious journals such as the Journal of Communication, Information, Communication & Society, and Social Media + Society. His notable works include "Second Screen and Participation: A Content Analysis on a Full Season Dataset of Tweets," which has been cited nearly 400 times, and "The open laboratory: Limits and possibilities of using Facebook, Twitter, and YouTube as a research data source," a seminal piece exploring the methodologies of social media research. His academic pursuits remain at the forefront of analyzing media manipulation and the spread of disinformation, particularly in the context of significant events like elections.

Beyond his publications, Professor Giglietto is actively involved in cutting-edge research projects that examine the role of social media in contemporary society. His recent work delves into the impact of coordinated link-sharing behavior and the spread of false information within hybrid news systems. Engaging with the academic community and the public, he frequently shares his insights through platforms like BlueSky and LinkedIn, where he discusses ongoing projects and the implications of his findings.

Committed to advancing the field of Internet Studies, Professor Giglietto continues to contribute to both theoretical and applied research, making significant strides in understanding the complex dynamics of digital communication. His work not only enriches academic discourse but also informs policy and practice in the digital age.

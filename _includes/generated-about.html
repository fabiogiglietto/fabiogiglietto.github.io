Fabio Giglietto, PhD, is a distinguished Professor of Internet Studies at the Department of Communication Sciences, Humanities and International Studies at the University of Urbino Carlo Bo. His academic pursuits predominantly explore the intricate dynamics between social systems, media, and digital technologies. With a robust focus on the theory of information, communication, and society, Professor Giglietto has made significant contributions to understanding how these elements influence contemporary societal interactions.

Professor Giglietto's scholarly work is widely recognized, with numerous publications in esteemed journals such as the Journal of Communication, Information, Communication & Society, and Social Media + Society. His research has been instrumental in examining the role of media and digital technologies in shaping public discourse, particularly his notable studies on social media analysis and disinformation. His work, "Second Screen and Participation: A Content Analysis on a Full Season Dataset of Tweets," which has garnered 399 citations, exemplifies his impact on the field.

Currently, Professor Giglietto is actively engaged in research on the manipulation of media within hybrid news systems and the spread of disinformation on digital platforms. His recent projects delve into coordinated behaviors in media link sharing, as evidenced by his publication, "It takes a village to manipulate the media: coordinated link sharing behavior during 2018 and 2019 Italian elections." His insights are frequently shared across professional networks, where he engages with the academic community and contributes to ongoing discussions on the challenges and opportunities presented by digital communication technologies.

Professor Giglietto continues to inspire through his teaching and research, fostering a deeper understanding of the digital landscape's societal implications. His dedication to advancing Internet Studies is reflected in his extensive body of work, which includes over 20 publications and a total of 1763 citations, underscoring his prominence as a leading figure in the field.
